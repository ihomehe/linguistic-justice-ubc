Understanding the Issue

What is Linguistic Justice?

Linguistic justice is the principle that no person should face discrimination, exclusion, or diminished opportunity because of the language they speak, the accent they carry, or the dialect they were raised with. Language hierarchies are not natural they are constructed, and they can be dismantled.

Linguistic Discrimination

Also called glottophobia or linguicism, linguistic discrimination is the unfair treatment of people based on their use of language including accent, dialect, vocabulary, or syntax. It is often invisible, dismissed as a preference rather than recognized as bias. At its most harmful, it closes doors in employment, healthcare, education, and belonging.

Discrimination based on accent can be harder to prove than other forms and that difficulty is itself part of the problem.

Accent-Based Bias

When listeners perceive a non-native accent, they may unconsciously associate it with lower intelligence, less authority, or reduced credibility, regardless of whether they understood everything that was said. Accent tells you nothing about what someone knows or what they are trying to communicate. But research shows it shapes how much listeners trust them, how seriously they take what is said, and whether they are offered opportunities.

Research from Concordia University (2022) found that foreign accents are associated with unfair treatment across multiple work contexts in Canada.

Linguistic Insecurity

When people repeatedly experience correction or dismissal based on how they sound rather than what they are saying, they develop linguistic insecurity. This is a self-consciousness about one's voice that leads to self-silencing, withdrawal from class participation, and a constant internal tax that monolingual speakers in dominant accents rarely pay. The communication often worked. The accent was still penalized.

Focus group data collected 2024, UBC Linguistic Justice Awareness Project.

Colonial Roots

In Canada, linguistic injustice has deep colonial roots. Residential schools forcibly stripped Indigenous children of their languages an act of cultural genocide whose effects persist today. English and French were imposed as official languages on lands where over 70 Indigenous languages were spoken. Linguistic justice requires acknowledging this history as its foundation.

Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada, Volume 5, 2015; Statistics Canada, 2021 Census.
"Linguistic justice is about awareness. It starts with the recognition that the way we judge someone's language says far more about us than it does about them and that awareness itself is a form of linguistic justice." Strang Burton, Associate Professor of Teaching, UBC Linguistics
Key Terms

Glossary

Linguistic justice has its own vocabulary. These are the terms used by researchers, educators, and advocates in this field. Understanding them is the first step toward being able to name what you experience and what you witness.

Discrimination or prejudice against individuals based on their language, accent, or dialect. The term was coined by French linguist Philippe Blanchet and is increasingly used alongside "linguicism" to describe a form of social discrimination that is often overlooked precisely because it is so normalized. Unlike racism or sexism, glottophobia rarely has legal protections against it.
Blanchet, P. (2016). Discriminations: combattre la glottophobie.
The practice of shifting between languages, dialects, or registers depending on social context. While often framed as a neutral skill, code-switching in the context of linguistic justice refers specifically to the labor required of non-dominant speakers to adapt their speech to dominant norms in order to be accepted, understood, or hired. This labor is unequally distributed and psychologically costly.
Young, V. A. (2009). "Nah, We Straight": An Argument Against Code Switching. JAC, 29(1/2).
A pedagogical and theoretical framework that views multilingual speakers not as operating in separate, bounded language systems, but as drawing fluidly from a unified linguistic repertoire. Translanguaging rejects the idea that switching between languages represents a deficit or a failure of competence. Instead, it recognizes the full complexity of multilingual meaning-making as an asset.
Garcia, O. & Wei, L. (2014). Translanguaging: Language, Bilingualism and Education.
A field of study that examines the intersection of race and language, exploring how racial ideologies shape the way language is perceived, evaluated, and used. Raciolinguistics argues that accent discrimination and language-based judgment cannot be separated from race: when someone's accent is marked as foreign or inferior, racial assumptions are almost always at work in that judgment.
Alim, H. S., Rickford, J. R. & Ball, A. F., eds. (2016). Raciolinguistics: How Language Shapes Our Ideas About Race.
The widespread belief that there is one correct, superior form of a language, and that deviations from it are errors. In Canada and most English-speaking institutions, this takes the form of privileging white, North American, educated English as the neutral default. Standard language ideology is the ideological foundation of accent discrimination: it naturalizes what is in fact a historically constructed hierarchy.
Lippi-Green, R. (2012). English with an Accent: Language, Ideology and Discrimination in the United States.
A state of self-consciousness about one's own language use, typically produced by repeated experiences of correction, misunderstanding, or social exclusion based on how one speaks. Linguistic insecurity is documented in Statistics Canada's 2022 survey: 31% of English speakers in Quebec and 24% of French speakers outside Quebec reported hesitating to use their minority language due to insecurity. At UBC, it manifests as self-silencing in classrooms and labs.
Statistics Canada, Survey on the Official Language Minority Population, 2022.
A practice and pedagogical approach that integrates multiple languages, dialects, and registers within a single piece of communication, including academic writing. Unlike code-switching, which implies alternating between separate systems, code-meshing treats multilingual expression as unified and legitimate. It challenges the expectation that academic writing must be conducted in Standard Academic English alone.
Young, V. A. & Martinez, A. Y., eds. (2011). Code-Meshing as World English: Pedagogy, Policy, Performance.
A framework that positions language as central to civic participation and democratic life. Linguistic citizenship argues that full participation in a society, including in education and public life, requires not just the right to speak, but the right to be heard in one's own language. Applied to the university, it asks: whose languages count as legitimate here, and who bears the cost of translation?
Stroud, C. (2001). African mother-tongue programmes and the politics of language. Journal of Multilingual and Multicultural Development.

Historical Context

A Timeline of Linguistic Injustice in Canada

Linguistic injustice at UBC does not exist in isolation. It is part of a longer history of language suppression, official language politics, and the slow, ongoing work of recognition and repair.

Pre-contact

Over 70 Indigenous Languages

Before European colonization, what is now Canada was home to a rich diversity of Indigenous language families, including Algonquian, Athabaskan, Salish, Iroquoian, and Inuktut. Multilingualism was common; many nations spoke several languages across trade and kinship networks.

1763

Royal Proclamation

The British Crown asserts sovereignty over vast Indigenous territories. English begins its institutionalization as the language of colonial administration, displacing Indigenous governance structures and the languages in which they operated.

1876

The Indian Act

Passed by the Canadian government, the Indian Act imposes sweeping controls on Indigenous peoples including restrictions on cultural practices and language. It creates the legal infrastructure for residential schools. Indigenous languages are increasingly treated as obstacles to assimilation rather than as complex, living knowledge systems.

1883–1996

Residential Schools

The Canadian government operates a network of residential schools in which Indigenous children are forcibly removed from their families and prohibited from speaking their languages. Children are punished for speaking their mother tongues. This systematic suppression is recognized by the Truth and Reconciliation Commission as cultural genocide. Over 150,000 children pass through the system. The last federally-run residential school closes in 1996.

1969

Official Languages Act

Canada declares English and French its two official languages, establishing bilingual rights in federal institutions. While framed as an equality measure, the Act entrenches a two-language hierarchy that does not recognize the over 70 Indigenous languages already present on these lands, or the hundreds of immigrant languages spoken by Canadians.

2015

Truth and Reconciliation Commission Calls to Action

The TRC releases 94 Calls to Action. Calls to Action 13, 14, and 15 specifically address Indigenous language and culture: the federal government must acknowledge that Indigenous rights include language rights, fund Indigenous language education, and appoint a languages commissioner to lead revitalization efforts.

2019

Indigenous Languages Act

Canada's Indigenous Languages Act receives royal assent, marking the first federal legislation specifically aimed at preserving, revitalizing, and promoting Indigenous languages. Critics note that while the Act is a step forward, it lacks binding funding commitments and does not fully reflect the distinctions between First Nations, Métis, and Inuit language needs.

2021

First Decline in Indigenous Language Speakers

Statistics Canada reports that the number of Indigenous people who can speak an Indigenous language falls for the first time since comparable data collection began in 1991. 237,420 speakers remain, down from 248,170 in 2016. Revitalization efforts are growing, but language loss continues to outpace them in many communities.

2022

Statistics Canada Documents Linguistic Discrimination

For the first time, the Survey on the Official Language Minority Population includes direct questions on linguistic insecurity and discrimination. Results show 1 in 4 adults in language minority populations have experienced discrimination based on language in the previous five years. The Canadian Social Survey also documents accent as a reported reason for discrimination among racialized Canadians.

2024

UBC Linguistic Justice Awareness Project

Eight focus groups are conducted at UBC Vancouver with students from over 15 countries. The research documents accent-based self-silencing, microaggressions, institutional barriers, and resilience across the student body. It is the first primary research on linguistic discrimination specifically at UBC Vancouver, beginning to fill a gap that the Student Diversity Census has not yet addressed.

The Project

About This Project

The Linguistic Justice Awareness Project is a research and awareness initiative based at the University of British Columbia, Faculty of Arts. It was developed to document student experiences of accent-based discrimination and linguistic marginalization at UBC and to use those findings to build a more inclusive campus.

Between September and November 2024, the project conducted eight focus groups with graduate and undergraduate students representing over fifteen countries and a wide range of disciplines. All sessions were facilitated by a Nigerian-born researcher who brought firsthand experience with accent-based discrimination to the facilitation, modeling vulnerability and generating deeper disclosure.

The project's outputs include a thematic analysis report, a short documentary film, and this website which synthesizes the research alongside national data on linguistic injustice in Canada. It sits within UBC's Strategic Equity and Anti-Racism (StEAR) Framework, which calls for identifying and dismantling systemic barriers to equity linguistic difference among them.

This work connects to a growing field at UBC: the PRISM Framework at UBC Okanagan examines plurilingualism and decolonization in the first-year writing classroom; the 2025 Speaker Series on Plurality, Linguistic Justice, and Decolonization brought fifteen scholars together to advance these conversations. This project contributes the student perspective gathered directly, in their own words.

For Those Living It

If you are experiencing linguistic discrimination

This page is full of data and institutional language. But before any of that, this section is for you specifically. For the student who lowered their voice. Who stopped putting their hand up. Who practiced a sentence alone before saying it out loud.

Your accent is not a barrier to your intelligence. It is evidence of a life lived across languages, cultures, and worlds that most people in this institution have never had to navigate.

Academic and professional environments have legitimate expectations around communication. What they do not have the right to do is confuse how you sound with how well you think. Those are different things, and the research is unambiguous on this point. The discomfort you feel is a rational response to a system that has collapsed that distinction and placed the cost of it on you.

You do not need to reduce your accent to deserve your place here. You already deserve it.

The self-silencing is real and you are not alone in it

In our focus groups, students from over 15 countries described the same experience: choosing not to speak in class, in labs, in meetings, not because they had nothing to say, but because they were afraid of how they would be received. If that is you, know that it is one of the most consistent findings in this research. You are not uniquely struggling. You are part of a majority that has been made to feel like a minority.

The internal pressure is a form of harm

Being told, even by well-meaning people, to take an accent reduction class, to speak more slowly, to enunciate more clearly these suggestions carry a message that your natural voice is a problem to be fixed. That message is wrong. Adapting how you speak to communicate more clearly is a choice that belongs to you, not an obligation imposed by others. The pressure you feel to change is a form of harm, even when the person applying it means well.

Your multilingualism is a form of intelligence

Operating in a second or third language requires cognitive flexibility, cultural agility, and a level of sustained effort that monolingual speakers rarely recognize or acknowledge. The research is clear on this. What is not always clear is whether the institutions you are operating within recognize it. This project exists, in part, to make that case.

You have options if you want to use them

If you have experienced discrimination based on your accent or language at UBC, you can report it. UBC's Equity and Inclusion Office offers confidential support and reporting pathways. You can also share your experience through the form on this site, which contributes to the evidence base without requiring you to identify yourself. You do not have to do anything but the options exist.

Allyship

What you can do as an ally

Linguistic justice is built in classrooms, labs, offices, and group chats every day, not only in policy documents. If you are not the person experiencing discrimination, here is what you can do depending on where you sit.

If you are a student

Notice when you ask someone to repeat themselves

Before asking, pause and ask: did the meaning come through? If it did, the communication worked, even if the accent was unfamiliar. The labor of being understood is rarely distributed equally. Try adjusting toward someone before asking them to come all the way to you.

If you are a student

Share this project with your class or department

The most immediate thing you can do is put this in front of more people. Send the link to a professor, post it in a group chat, bring it up in a seminar. Awareness spreads person by person.

If you are a student

Examine your own assumptions about "good English"

Most of us have internalized a hierarchy of accents which ones sound educated, which ones sound foreign, which ones sound authoritative. That hierarchy was taught to you. It can be unlearned. Start by noticing it.

If you are an instructor

Audit your assessment language

Look at your rubrics. Do they reward Standard Academic English as the default? Consider whether the criteria you use to assess "clarity" or "communication" inadvertently penalize students who are doing their most complex intellectual work in a language that is not their first.

If you are an instructor

Never correct a student's accent mid-sentence

Interrupting a student to correct how they said something rather than what they said teaches them that the way they speak is more important than what they are trying to communicate. It isn't. Focus on content first, always.

If you are an instructor

Download and use the instructor guide

The guide includes discussion questions, classroom activities, and further reading designed specifically for bringing this project into your teaching. It takes five minutes to download and can open an hour of meaningful conversation.

If you are a supervisor

Distinguish between clarity and correctness

Your supervisee may communicate differently than you do. That is not the same as communicating poorly. There are legitimate expectations around academic communication, but accent is not one of them. Before correcting word choice or phrasing, ask whether the meaning came through. If it did, the communication worked, and the correction can wait or be dropped entirely.

If you are a supervisor

Create space before you correct

Focus group participants described supervisors who interrupted frequently to correct word choice as a source of significant anxiety. Consider waiting until a natural pause, framing feedback as a question rather than a correction, and separating language feedback from content feedback so students can hear both.

If you are anyone

Learn something in a language spoken around you

Even a few words in Mandarin, Punjabi, Farsi, or Tagalog languages spoken by thousands of people on this campus is a gesture that says: your language has value here, not just your English. It matters more than you might think.

Institutional Asks

What UBC Can Do

Awareness is necessary but not sufficient. These are concrete, actionable recommendations drawn from the focus group data and existing literature. They are directed at UBC as an institution.

01

Measure what is missing

Add questions about linguistic discrimination and accent-based experiences to the Student Diversity Census. UBC currently tracks languages spoken, but has no data on how those languages affect student experience. You cannot address what you do not measure.

Directed at: Planning & Institutional Research, Equity & Inclusion Office
02

Reform lecture capture policy

Create a formal accommodation pathway for students who experience significant anxiety about being recorded in a second or third language. The current policy requires all participating students to use a microphone, with no flexibility. One focus group participant described being hurt and excluded by the institution's refusal to offer an alternative. A simple policy adjustment could prevent this.

Directed at: UBC Centre for Teaching, Learning and Technology; individual instructors
03

Train supervisors on language-supportive feedback

Graduate supervisors and lab PIs often provide the most sustained academic relationship for international students. Focus group data shows that well-intentioned but interruptive correction of word choice compounds linguistic anxiety, particularly in lab environments where power differentials are high. UBC should include language-supportive feedback practices in supervisor training programs.

Directed at: Faculty of Graduate and Postdoctoral Studies, individual supervisors
04

Name linguistic justice in equity frameworks

UBC's StEAR Framework and Inclusion Action Plan address racism, ableism, and gender discrimination but do not explicitly name linguistic discrimination or accent-based bias. Naming it is not symbolic: it creates the institutional language needed for students to report experiences, for instructors to recognize and address it, and for the university to be held accountable for it.

Directed at: Equity & Inclusion Office, StEAR Framework governance
05

Recognize multilingualism as a credential

UBC's graduate programs require English proficiency scores as a threshold for entry, but do not recognize multilingualism as an academic asset in admissions or assessment. Institutional recognition that students operating across two or three languages daily are performing significant cognitive labor, not demonstrating a deficit, would shift the frame from remediation to recognition.

Directed at: Admissions, individual faculties and programs
06

Support Indigenous language learning on campus

Any institutional commitment to linguistic justice must begin with Indigenous languages. UBC should expand Indigenous language course offerings, fund Indigenous language programming through the First Nations Languages Program, and actively integrate Indigenous language learning into its land acknowledgment practice rather than treating acknowledgment as a standalone gesture.

Directed at: UBC First Nations Languages Program, Senior Administration
These recommendations were developed from primary focus group research conducted at UBC Vancouver in 2024 and from the existing scholarly literature on linguistic justice in higher education. They are offered as a starting point for institutional dialogue, not as a final or exhaustive list.
