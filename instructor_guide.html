This guide is designed to accompany the Linguistic Justice Awareness Project at UBC — including the interview documentary and the project website. It is suitable for first-year writing courses, ESL/EAP classes, equity and inclusion workshops, graduate seminars, and student orientation programming.

Two versions of the documentary are available: a 3-minute awareness version for time-limited settings, and a 15-minute extended version for sessions with discussion time. Both can be found at LinguisticJustice.UBC.ca.

Discussion questions, activities, and readings are organized so you can use as much or as little as fits your context. You do not need to use everything.

Before You Begin

Preparing for the conversation

Take a few minutes before class to hold these questions yourself. You are not required to share your answers aloud, but they will shape how you facilitate.
  • How many students in your class speak English as a first language? How many are operating in English as a second or third language?
  • What assumptions are embedded in your assessment criteria around "clarity," "academic voice," or "standard English"? Whose English do those criteria center?
  • Have you ever asked a student to repeat themselves? What prompted it — genuine confusion, or something else?
  • When you give feedback on student writing or speaking, do you distinguish between clarity and correctness? Should you?
  • What languages are spoken around you in your department, your building, your faculty? Do you know?
Discussion Questions

After the 3-minute version

These questions work well for a 20–30 minute discussion following the short version of the documentary.
Question 1

What stayed with you from what you just watched? What surprised you?

Follow-up: Was there a moment, a face, or a phrase that you are still thinking about? What was it about that moment?
Question 2

Has your accent, dialect, or way of speaking ever affected how others responded to you — positively or negatively? You are invited to share, but not required to.

Follow-up: If you have not experienced this yourself, have you witnessed it happening to someone else? What did you do?
Question 3

The project is titled "Your Voice Belongs Here." What would it actually mean for every voice to belong in this classroom — not just be tolerated, but genuinely belong?

Follow-up: What is one concrete thing that would need to change in this room for that to be true?
Question 4

Have you ever adjusted how you speak based on who you were talking to? What did that feel like?

Follow-up: Is that adjustment always a choice, or does it sometimes feel compelled? What makes the difference?
Discussion Questions

After the 15-minute version

These questions are designed for a 45–60 minute discussion. They can also be used as written reflection prompts if discussion feels too exposing for some students.
Question 1

One participant described feeling like "two different people" in two different languages — funny and expressive in Spanish, but guarded and grammatically anxious in English. What do you think they meant? Have you experienced anything similar?

Follow-up: What does it cost someone to leave part of themselves at the door when they enter an academic space?
Question 2

A participant was told by a well-meaning peer to take an "accent reduction class" so that opportunities would come more easily. Why might well-meaning advice still cause harm? What would a genuinely supportive response have looked like?

Follow-up: What does this tell us about the difference between individual kindness and structural change?
Question 3

Several participants described going silent in class — not because they had nothing to say, but because they were afraid of how they would be received. How might this silence be misread by instructors? What are the academic and professional consequences of that misreading?

Follow-up: What responsibility do instructors and peers have when they notice a student going quiet?
Question 4

The data shows that listing multilingual fluency on a resume does not improve job callback rates in Canada — name-based bias operates independently of language skills. What does this tell us about how multilingualism is actually valued in Canadian society versus how it is claimed to be valued?

Follow-up: How does this affect how you think about the purpose of a university education?
Question 5

One participant refused to use a microphone during recorded lectures because they did not want to be captured making a grammar mistake. The institution declined to offer an alternative. Was that the right call? What accommodation would you have offered?

Follow-up: What does this example reveal about who institutional policies are designed around?
Question 6

Strang Burton says "awareness itself is a form of linguistic justice." What do you think he means? Do you agree? Is awareness enough?

Follow-up: What comes after awareness? What does action look like at the level of an individual, a classroom, a department, an institution?
Classroom Activities

Activities and exercises

Activities range from 10 to 30 minutes. They can be used in combination or independently.
Activity 1 · 15–20 minutes

Language Autobiography

Students write a short reflection (one paragraph) responding to: "What languages do you speak, and what role do they play in your life?"

  1. Give students 8 minutes to write individually, in whatever language feels most natural.
  2. Students share in pairs for 5 minutes.
  3. Debrief as a full group: What patterns emerged? What surprised you about your partner's experience? What did you learn about the room you are in?

Note: students who speak only one language often find this exercise unexpectedly revealing — they realize they had not thought about their language as a language until now.

Activity 2 · 10 minutes

The Listening Experiment

Play two short audio clips of the same content read in two different accents — one that matches the dominant accent in your context, one that does not.

  1. After each clip, students write down three words describing their first impression of the speaker.
  2. Compare word lists as a group. What patterns appear?
  3. Ask: where did these impressions come from? Are they fair? Are they accurate?

This exercise makes accent bias tangible without requiring anyone to disclose personal experience.

Activity 3 · 20–25 minutes

Rewriting the Rubric

Take a standard assignment rubric used in your course and examine it for assumptions about language.

  1. In small groups, identify any criteria that privilege Standard Academic English as a default — words like "clear," "coherent," "professional," or "appropriate" that may be doing ideological work without naming it.
  2. For each criterion identified, ask: what is this criterion actually trying to assess? Can we assess that without defaulting to one form of English?
  3. Groups draft alternative language for one criterion and share with the class.

This works best when instructors participate genuinely and are willing to revise their own rubrics in response.

Activity 4 · Homework / 300 words

Data Response

Students choose one statistic from the Linguistic Justice website's Data section and research it further.

In 300 words, they answer: What is the context behind this number? Who is affected and how? What would need to change for this number to be different? What would it mean if it were zero?

This works well as a bridge between the affective experience of watching the documentary and more analytical academic engagement with the evidence.

Activity 5 · Ongoing

Share Your Story

Encourage students to visit the "Share Your Story" section of LinguisticJustice.UBC.ca and submit their own experiences using the anonymous form.

Their submissions contribute to ongoing research that the project team uses to build the evidence base for institutional change. Students who submit are participating in something that extends beyond the classroom.

Further Reading

Scholarship and resources

A starting point, not an exhaustive list. Organized from most accessible to most specialist.
Bouchard, Marie-Eve (2022)
"Doing linguistics is doing social justice work." UBC Faculty of Arts. arts.ubc.ca
Accessible and brief. A good starting point for students new to the connection between linguistics and equity.
Young, Vershawn Ashanti (2009)
"Nah, We Straight": An Argument Against Code Switching. JAC: A Journal of Composition Theory, 29(1/2), 49–76.
Foundational text on why code-switching places unfair burden on non-dominant speakers. Essential reading for writing instructors.
Lippi-Green, Rosina (2012)
English with an Accent: Language, Ideology and Discrimination in the United States. Routledge.
Comprehensive examination of standard language ideology and how it operates in education, employment, and media.
Alim, H. Samy, Rickford, John R., and Ball, Arnetha F., eds. (2016)
Raciolinguistics: How Language Shapes Our Ideas About Race. Oxford University Press.
Establishes the field of raciolinguistics. Particularly relevant for understanding accent bias in relation to race.
Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada (2015)
Calls to Action. National Centre for Truth and Reconciliation. nctr.ca
Calls to Action 13, 14, and 15 specifically address Indigenous language rights. Essential for contextualizing linguistic justice on unceded territory.
Garcia, Ofelia and Wei, Li (2014)
Translanguaging: Language, Bilingualism and Education. Palgrave Macmillan.
Foundational text on translanguaging as a pedagogical and theoretical framework. Useful for instructors who want to move from deficit to asset models.
UBC PRISM Framework (2025)
Plurality, Linguistic Justice, and Decolonization: Addressing Current Issues in Teaching First-year Writing to Multilingual Learners. fccs.ok.ubc.ca/linguistic-justice/
UBC Okanagan's developing framework for classroom application of linguistic justice principles. Includes practical tools for instructors.
